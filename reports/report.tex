\begin{abstract}
We investigate learned decoding of a rate-$1/2$, constraint-length-7
convolutional code under periodic sinusoidal interference, motivated by
industrial IoT channels.  Three classical baselines are first evaluated:
mismatched Viterbi (B1), oracle Viterbi (B2), and FFT-based interference
cancellation (B5).  At SNR~$=5$~dB and INR~$=5$~dB, B1 saturates at
BLER~$=1.0$, B2 achieves $10^{-4}$, and B5 achieves $0.35$.
An end-to-end BiGRU decoder (N1) fails to learn the task --- an information
bottleneck prevents implicit trellis search --- and exceeds the $2.1$~MFLOP
compute constraint by $3.17\times$.
A neural branch metric estimator (N2) resolves this by keeping Viterbi
intact and replacing only the branch metric computation with a small BiGRU
($h=16$, $913{,}664$ MACs).  N2 (seed~32) achieves BLER~$=2.5\times10^{-3}$
at the same operating point, a gain exceeding five SNR~dB over B1 and
$\approx1.2$~dB from the oracle by log-linear interpolation.
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

Classical convolutional decoding via the Viterbi algorithm assumes a
precisely known channel model.  In AWGN-only environments, mismatched
branch metrics computed under this assumption achieve near-optimal
block-error-rate (BLER) performance.  However, in industrial and
IoT settings, narrowband sinusoidal interference from motor drives,
power converters, and switching regulators regularly coexists with
thermal noise~\cite{viterbi1967,proakis2008}.  A standard Viterbi
decoder that ignores such interference treats the tonal energy as
additional noise, causing severe BLER degradation at moderate
interference-to-noise ratios (INR).

This report investigates the following question:
\emph{Can a computationally constrained learned component, inserted into
an otherwise classical Viterbi decoder, recover most of the performance
lost to sinusoidal interference?}

We pursue this question in three phases:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Phase~1 --- Classical Baselines.}  We implement and
    evaluate the mismatched Viterbi (B1), the oracle Viterbi with perfect
    interference knowledge (B2), and an FFT-based interference cancellation
    baseline (B5).  These establish the performance floor, ceiling, and a
    signal-processing mid-point.

  \item \textbf{Phase~2a --- End-to-End Neural Decoder (Negative Result).}
    A bidirectional GRU is trained to map raw received samples directly to
    information bits.  The architecture fails to learn meaningful decoding,
    plateauing near random-guessing accuracy.  Analysis reveals two root
    causes: an information bottleneck at the pooling layer and the
    intractability of implicitly representing a 64-state Viterbi search
    within a small network.  The model also violates the $2.1$~MFLOP
    compute constraint.

  \item \textbf{Phase~2b --- Neural Branch Metric Estimator.}
    Motivated by the Phase~2a failure, we decompose the decoding task:
    a small BiGRU learns local per-step branch metrics capturing
    interference effects; the Viterbi algorithm performs the global
    trellis search using those metrics.  This architecture succeeds,
    achieving BLER~$=2.5\times10^{-3}$ at SNR~$=5$~dB,
    INR~$=5$~dB, well within the compute budget.
\end{enumerate}

The key thesis is that \emph{problem decomposition} --- assigning local
channel estimation to a neural network and global sequence search to a
classical algorithm --- is the critical design principle enabling
efficient and effective decoding under structured interference.

\section{System Model}

\subsection{Channel}

The discrete-time channel model is
\begin{equation}
  r[t] = x[t] + n[t] + i[t], \quad t = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1,
  \label{eq:channel}
\end{equation}
where $x[t] \in \{-1, +1\}$ is a BPSK-modulated coded symbol,
$n[t] \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ is additive white Gaussian noise,
and $i[t]$ is a deterministic sinusoidal interferer:
\begin{equation}
  i[t] = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi t}{P} + \varphi\right).
  \label{eq:interference}
\end{equation}
The amplitude $A$ is derived from the interference-to-noise ratio (INR):
\begin{equation}
  A = \sqrt{2 \cdot 10^{\mathrm{INR}_{\mathrm{dB}}/10} \cdot \sigma^2},
  \label{eq:amplitude}
\end{equation}
which ensures that the average sinusoidal power equals
$10^{\mathrm{INR}_{\mathrm{dB}}/10} \cdot \sigma^2$.
The period $P$ is drawn uniformly from $\{8, 9, \ldots, 32\}$ samples,
the phase $\varphi$ is drawn uniformly from $[0, 2\pi)$, and the
noise variance is $\sigma^2 = 10^{-\mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{dB}}/10}$
(with unit symbol energy $E_s = 1$).

\subsection{Trellis Code}

The encoder is the NASA standard rate-$1/2$, constraint-length-7
convolutional code with octal generator polynomials
$G_1 = 171_8 = (1111001)_2$ and $G_2 = 133_8 = (1011011)_2$~\cite{nasastandard}.
The constraint length $K_c = 7$ gives memory $m = K_c - 1 = 6$ and
$S = 2^m = 64$ trellis states.  The free distance is $d_{\mathrm{free}} = 10$.

Encoding processes $K = 256$ information bits.  Six tail (zero) bits are
appended to terminate the trellis back to state~0, giving $K + m = 262$
total input bits and $N = 2 \times 262 = 524$ coded bits per block.
The effective code rate is $R_{\mathrm{eff}} = K/N = 256/524 \approx 0.489$.

\subsection{Compute Budget}
\label{sec:budget}

A compute constraint of $2.1 \times 10^6$ multiply-accumulate operations
(MACs) per block is enforced for all methods.  All profiling uses a
consistent MAC-based convention throughout: one MAC counts one
multiply-add pair (e.g., a squared difference $(y-x)^2$ is one MAC, not
two separate operations).  The budget is anchored to the cost of the
Viterbi algorithm profiled under this convention.

The Viterbi add-compare-select (ACS) loop uses $N = 512$ coded bits
(the $K = 256$ information bits at rate~$1/2$, excluding tail sections
for this budget calculation).  There are $T = N/2 = 256$ trellis steps,
$S = 64$ destination states, 2 incoming branches per state, and
2 output symbols per branch, each requiring 1~MAC:
\begin{equation}
  C_{\mathrm{Viterbi}} = T \times S \times 2 \times 2
  = 256 \times 64 \times 4 = 65{,}536 \text{ MACs}.
  \label{eq:viterbi_cost}
\end{equation}
The $2.1$~MFLOP budget therefore allows roughly $32\times$ the cost of
the Viterbi ACS loop, providing generous headroom for a neural component
while remaining tractable for edge deployment.  All methods reported in
this work are verified to satisfy this constraint.

\section{Phase 1: Classical Baselines}

\subsection{Mismatched Viterbi (B1)}

The mismatched Viterbi decoder assumes an AWGN-only channel.  At trellis
step $t$, the branch metric for the transition $s \to s'$ producing BPSK
hypothesis $\mathbf{x}_{s \to s'} \in \{-1,+1\}^2$ is
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_{\mathrm{B1}}(s \to s', k) =
    -\frac{\norm{\mathbf{y}[k] - \mathbf{x}_{s \to s'}}^2}{2\sigma^2},
  \label{eq:b1metric}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}[k] = (r[2k],\, r[2k+1])^T$ denotes the received pair
at trellis step $k$ (distinct from the scalar sample $r[t]$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:channel}).
Because the interference term $i[t]$ is not modeled, B1 treats sinusoidal
energy as noise, causing BLER saturation even at moderate INR.

\subsection{Oracle Viterbi (B2)}

The oracle Viterbi has full knowledge of $A$, $P$, and $\varphi$ and
subtracts the interference before computing branch metrics:
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_{\mathrm{B2}}(s \to s', k) =
    -\frac{\norm{\mathbf{y}[k] - \mathbf{x}_{s \to s'} - \mathbf{i}[k]}^2}{2\sigma^2},
  \label{eq:b2metric}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{i}[k] = (i[2k],\, i[2k+1])^T$ is the known interference pair at trellis step $k$.
B2 represents the performance ceiling: it achieves optimal ML decoding
under the true channel model and serves as the upper bound for all methods.

\subsection{Interference Cancellation + Viterbi (B5)}

B5 estimates the interference parameters from the received signal and
applies a subtraction prior to mismatched Viterbi decoding:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mathbf{y}}[k] = \mathbf{y}[k] - \hat{A}\begin{pmatrix}\sin(2\pi(2k)/\hat{P}+\hat{\varphi})\\\sin(2\pi(2k{+}1)/\hat{P}+\hat{\varphi})\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
followed by the B1 branch metric on $\tilde{\mathbf{y}}[k]$.

The estimation pipeline proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute the periodogram of $r[t]$ via FFT.
  \item Identify the top-5 magnitude peaks whose frequencies fall within
    the valid range $[1/P_{\max}, 1/P_{\min}]$.
  \item For each candidate frequency $\omega_k = 2\pi f_k$, fit
    $r[t] \approx a_k \cos(\omega_k t) + b_k \sin(\omega_k t) + c$
    via least squares: $\hat{\mathbf{c}}_k = (\mathbf{A}_k^T \mathbf{A}_k)^{-1} \mathbf{A}_k^T \mathbf{r}$.
  \item Recover amplitude and phase:
    $\hat{A}_k = \sqrt{a_k^2 + b_k^2}$, $\hat{\varphi}_k = \mathrm{atan2}(a_k, b_k)$.
  \item Select the candidate minimizing the residual
    $\sum_t (r[t] - \hat{A}_k \sin(\omega_k t + \hat{\varphi}_k))^2$.
\end{enumerate}
B5 demonstrates the limit of signal-processing-only interference mitigation.

\section{Phase 2a: End-to-End Neural Decoder}
\label{sec:phase2a}

\subsection{Architecture}

Motivated by prior work on RNN-based channel decoders~\cite{gruber2017,
kim2018}, we implemented a bidirectional GRU (BiGRU) end-to-end decoder,
denoted N1.  The architecture is:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Input:} $(B, 524, 1)$ --- raw received samples, one per
    time step.
  \item \textbf{BiGRU:} hidden size $h=32$ per direction, bidirectional,
    1 layer.  Output: $(B, 524, 64)$ all-timestep hidden states.
  \item \textbf{Pooling:} Final hidden states from both directions
    concatenated: $(B, 64)$.
  \item \textbf{Output head:} Linear$(64, 256) \to (B, 256)$ logits.
\end{itemize}

Total parameter count: $\approx 23{,}000$.  Three pooling strategies
were evaluated: mean pooling over all $524$ time steps ($\approx52.1\%$
bit accuracy), final-hidden-state pooling ($\approx53.5\%$), and
attention-weighted pooling ($\approx53.8\%$).

\subsection{Training Configuration}

Training used binary cross-entropy with logits (BCEWithLogitsLoss), the
Adam optimizer at lr~$=10^{-3}$ with StepLR decay, batch size 128,
and on-the-fly batch generation at SNR~$\in[0,10]$~dB,
INR~$\in[-5,15]$~dB, $P\in[8,32]$.
Validation BLER was measured every 5 epochs on 1{,}000 blocks at
SNR~$=5$~dB, INR~$=5$~dB; early stopping used patience 20.

\subsection{Analytical Compute Budget}
\label{sec:n1flops}

The BiGRU with $h=32$, input dimension $d=1$, applied to the full
$T=524$-sample sequence incurs the following FLOPs per block.
Each GRU gate (input, reset, update) requires $h \times (h + d) = 32 \times 33$
multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations; there are 3 gates, giving
$6 \times h \times (h + d)$ MACs per time step per direction:
\begin{align}
  C_{\mathrm{GRU,dir}} &= T \times 6 \times h \times (h + d) \notag \\
  &= 524 \times 6 \times 32 \times 33 = 3{,}320{,}064.
\end{align}
Both directions together, plus the linear output head:
\begin{align}
  C_{\mathrm{N1}} &= 2 \times C_{\mathrm{GRU,dir}} + 64 \times 256 \notag \\
  &= 6{,}640{,}128 + 16{,}384 \approx 6.66 \text{ MFLOP}.
\end{align}
This is \textbf{$3.17\times$ the $2.1$~MFLOP constraint}, making N1
inadmissible under the compute budget even if it had learned to decode.

\subsection{Experimental Results}

Across all training configurations and pooling strategies, the model
plateaued at approximately $53.5\%$ bit accuracy, only marginally above
the $50\%$ random-guessing baseline.  BLER remained at $1.0$ throughout
all training runs --- no block was ever decoded correctly.

An overfitting sanity check (4 fixed blocks, 500 epochs) confirmed that
the architecture has sufficient capacity to memorize small datasets
(bit accuracy $100\%$, loss $<0.003$), ruling out implementation errors.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

Two structural problems preclude N1 from succeeding:

\paragraph{Information bottleneck.}
The pooling operation compresses the full $524$-step sequence into a
$64$-dimensional vector, from which $256$ information bits must be
recovered.  The mutual information of a BPSK convolutional codeword
transmitted over this channel is far greater than what a $64$-dim vector
can represent; the bottleneck discards the structured redundancy that
Viterbi exploits through its trellis search.

\paragraph{Implicit trellis search.}
Viterbi exploits the Markov structure of the code via dynamic programming
over $64$ states across $262$ trellis sections.  For N1 to succeed, the
GRU hidden state would need to implicitly represent this exponential-space
search --- a combinatorially intractable mapping for a network with
$\approx23{,}000$ parameters.

\paragraph{Design lesson.}
The failure of N1 motivates a strict decomposition: assign the problem
of \emph{local channel estimation} (what is the likely interference at
time $t$?) to the neural component, and the problem of
\emph{global sequence search} (what path through the trellis best
explains the full received sequence?) to the Viterbi algorithm.
This decomposition is the foundation of Phase~2b.

\section{Phase 2b: Neural Branch Metric Estimator}
\label{sec:phase2b}

\subsection{Motivation}

The Viterbi algorithm decomposes the MAP sequence detection problem into
a dynamic program over branch metrics $\gamma(s \to s', t)$.  In the
oracle decoder (B2), these metrics are exact log-likelihoods under the
true channel.  The key insight is that the oracle metric requires
knowledge of $A$, $P$, and $\varphi$ --- three continuously-valued
parameters that change per block --- whereas Viterbi itself is a
deterministic algorithm that requires only a metric table.

N2 replaces the closed-form oracle metric computation with a BiGRU that
\emph{learns} to predict those metrics from the received signal, without
ever explicitly estimating $A$, $P$, or $\varphi$.

\subsection{Architecture}

The neural branch metric estimator processes received signals grouped
into pairs, aligned with the trellis time axis.  The full architecture
is as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Input:} $(B, T, 2)$ with $T = K + m = 262$.  Consecutive
    received samples are grouped into pairs $\mathbf{y}[k] = (r[2k],\, r[2k+1])^T$.
  \item \textbf{BiGRU:} hidden size $h=16$ per direction, 1 layer.
    Output: $(B, T, 32)$ --- both-direction hidden states concatenated.
  \item \textbf{Linear head:} Linear$(32, 4) \to (B, T, 4)$ --- one score
    per BPSK output pair at each trellis step.
\end{itemize}

The four BPSK output pairs correspond to all combinations
$\mathbf{h}_j \in \{-1,+1\}^2$:
$\mathbf{h}_0 = (-1,-1)$, $\mathbf{h}_1 = (-1,+1)$,
$\mathbf{h}_2 = (+1,-1)$, $\mathbf{h}_3 = (+1,+1)$.
During Viterbi, the score for transition $s \to s'$ at step $t$ is
looked up as $\hat{\gamma}[t, j(s \to s')]$, where $j(s \to s')$ is
the precomputed index of the BPSK pair produced by that transition.

Total parameter count: $\approx 1{,}860$ --- approximately $12.5\times$
fewer parameters than N1.

\subsection{Oracle Training Targets and Loss Function}

For each training sample, the true interference $\mathbf{i}[t]$ is
known (generated on-the-fly during training).  The oracle branch metric
for hypothesis $\mathbf{h}_j$ at step $t$ is the negative squared
Euclidean distance:
\begin{equation}
  \gamma^*_j(k) = -\norm{\mathbf{y}[k] - \mathbf{h}_j - \mathbf{i}[k]}^2.
  \label{eq:oracle_metric}
\end{equation}
This is the unnormalized oracle log-likelihood; the $1/(2\sigma^2)$
scaling is applied at inference time after the NN forward pass.

The network is trained with mean squared error (MSE) loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{B \cdot T \cdot 4}
    \sum_{b,t,j} \bigl( \hat{\gamma}_{b,j}(t) - \gamma^*_{b,j}(t) \bigr)^2.
\end{equation}

MSE on the oracle metrics (rather than cross-entropy on information bits)
is the natural loss for this regression task and is the approach
adopted by ViterbiNet~\cite{shlezinger2020} and related hybrid
model-based/data-driven architectures.

Two random seeds were trained with different oracle metric normalizations:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Seed~42 (normalized targets):}  Training targets include
    the $1/(2\sigma^2)$ factor; the NN is trained to predict the full
    log-likelihood.  No post-processing is applied at inference.

  \item \textbf{Seed~32 (external normalization):}  Training targets are
    the raw unnormalized distances $-\norm{\mathbf{y}[k] - \mathbf{h}_j -
    \mathbf{i}[k]}^2$.  At inference, the NN output is divided by $2\sigma^2$
    before passing to Viterbi.  This decouples the NN's learning task
    (estimating interference-aware distances) from the noise-level
    scaling, which is known at inference time.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Viterbi Integration}

The N2 decode pipeline proceeds in two stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{NN forward pass:} A single forward pass through the
    BiGRU produces the branch metric table $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Gamma}} \in
    \mathbb{R}^{T \times 4}$ for the entire block.  For seed~32,
    this is divided element-wise by $2\sigma^2$.
  \item \textbf{Viterbi ACS:} Standard add-compare-select is applied
    using $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Gamma}}$, with the precomputed
    transition-to-output-index table $j(s \to s')$.  Traceback
    recovers the $K = 256$ decoded information bits.
\end{enumerate}

The NN forward pass and the Viterbi step are fully separable, enabling
efficient implementation.  No per-step NN calls are required.

\subsection{Compute Cost}

The analytical FLOPs for N2 with $h=16$, $d=2$, $T=262$ are:
\begin{align}
  C_{\mathrm{GRU,dir}} &= 262 \times 6 \times 16 \times (16+2) = 453{,}888,\\
  C_{\mathrm{N2}} &= 2 \times 453{,}888 + 262 \times 32 \times 4
    = 907{,}776 + 33{,}536 \approx 941{,}312 \text{ FLOPs}.
\end{align}

\subsection{Seed~32 vs.\ Seed~42}

Both seeds use an identical BiGRU architecture; they differ only in
the formulation of the oracle training target:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Seed~32} learns unnormalized oracle distances
    $-\norm{\mathbf{y}[k]-\mathbf{h}_j-\mathbf{i}[k]}^2$ and applies the
    noise scaling $1/(2\sigma^2)$ deterministically at inference.
    This isolates the NN's learning task to \emph{interference-aware
    channel estimation}, while noise normalization is handled analytically.
  \item \textbf{Seed~42} learns the full log-likelihood
    $-\norm{\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{h}_j-\mathbf{i}}^2 / (2\sigma^2)$,
    requiring the NN to also capture how the noise variance rescales
    the metric.  The resulting target distribution has a much larger
    dynamic range (initial MSE~$=122.4$ vs.\ $24.4$ for seed~32),
    slowing convergence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations of the Current Approach}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Trellis is fixed.}  The NASA K=7 code was not designed
    for sinusoidal interference; the trellis structure may not be optimal.
    Phase~3 will address this via evolutionary trellis search.
  \item \textbf{Distribution shift.}  Training covers $P \in [8,32]$;
    performance on $P \notin [8,32]$ has not been characterized.
  % \item \textbf{Wall-clock latency.}  The $\approx2$-second per-block
  %   latency of N2 in unoptimized Python inference is impractical;
  %   GPU batching or C-compiled inference would reduce this by orders
  %   of magnitude.
  \item \textbf{Single interference model.}  Only sinusoidal interference
    was considered; generalization to other structured interference
    (e.g., chirp, pulsed) is an open question.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This report documents three phases of research toward robust,
compute-efficient decoding under sinusoidal interference.

Phase~1 established that classical mismatched Viterbi (B1) saturates at
BLER~$=1$ under moderate interference (INR~$\geq5$~dB), while the oracle
(B2) maintains BLER~$=10^{-4}$ regardless of INR.  An FFT-based
interference cancellation baseline (B5) provides partial mitigation but
degrades sharply above INR~$\approx0$~dB due to estimation error.

Phase~2a demonstrated that an end-to-end BiGRU decoder (N1) cannot learn
to decode the K=7 code, plateauing near random-guessing accuracy.  The
root causes are an information bottleneck at the pooling layer and the
intractability of implicitly representing a 64-state Viterbi search.
N1 also exceeds the $2.1$~MFLOP compute budget by $3.17\times$.

Phase~2b introduced the neural branch metric estimator (N2), which
preserves the Viterbi dynamic-programming structure and replaces only the
branch metric computation.  N2 (seed~32) achieves BLER~$=2.5\times10^{-3}$
at SNR~$=5$~dB, INR~$=5$~dB --- a gain exceeding five SNR dB over B1
and within $\approx1.2$~dB of the oracle B2 (by log-linear interpolation
of the B2 curve to the matched BLER) --- using $43.5\%$ of the
$2.1$~MFLOP budget.  The key insight is that \emph{decomposing} local
channel estimation (neural) from global trellis search (classical
algorithm) is the critical design principle.

\paragraph{Future work (Phase~3).}
The trellis itself was fixed to the NASA K=7 code throughout this work.
Phase~3 will co-design the trellis via evolutionary search, optimizing
the 64-state transition table jointly with the neural branch metric
estimator under the sinusoidal interference channel.  The hypothesis
is that a searched trellis will provide an additional $\geq1$~dB gain
over the classical K=7 code when decoded with N2, and that this gain
will not be achievable by a random trellis --- validating that the
search is non-trivial.
